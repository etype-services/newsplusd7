12.10.17 News Briefs

​

Next session of Professor’s Corner slated for Mon., Dec. 11 in Denton

 

Session title: “Adapting Homer’s Odyssey, or What Makes an Instant Classic?”

Presenter:   Dr. Gretchen Busl, Texas Woman’s University

Date and location: Monday, December 11, 2017,  7:00-8:30 p.m. at the Denton South Branch Library

 

This session draws inspiration from Italo Calvino’s “Why Read the Classics?,” in which he gives a number of striking definitions of a “classic” text. The group will concentrate on one of these, the idea that “classics are those books that arrive to us carrying the traces of the readings that precede ours, and behind them bear the traces they themselves have left on the culture or cultures through which they have passed.”  The significance of these texts is not simply what lies on the page, but also what has passed into the “generally circulated memory” in relation to these texts. His definition of  a “classic” implies that a work is encoded not only with the texts which precede it, but with all the interpretations of that work that have come after it, including works which may refer to it, re-create it, or adapt it. In fact, as Calvino writes, “every first reading of a classic is in reality a rereading.” Even the first reading of a text which has entered the collective cultural understanding will bring with it a sense of familiarity.

 

Drawing from this premise, the group will use The Odyssey and adaptations like The Penelopiad and the Tidewater Tales to explore how, by incorporating “classic” texts through adaptation and appropriation, a novel can instantly become the kind of classic for which even the first reading is a re-reading.

 

Dr. Gretchen Busl earned her Ph.D. in Literature at the University of Notre Dame with a focus on adaptation and translation and a minor in Gender Studies. She is currently Assistant Professor of English at Texas Woman’s University, where she teaches a variety of courses in global literature, women's literature, literary theory, and writing.

 

This program is free and open to the public. Refreshments will be served.

 The reading made available in advance by the presenter can be obtained from the series coordinator if it is not attached to this e-mail or if links are not provided above. Reading in advance is encouraged but not required.

The purpose of this free series is to meet a public need for high quality presentations on literary topics by having local English professors talk about their special interests.  The presentations are aimed at the general public and allow for discussion.  Readings of modest length are usually available in advance.  Gatherings are usually on the second Monday of specified months (new this season)  from 7:00 p.m. to about 8:30 p.m. Monthly announcements are available by e-mail; to get on the mailing list, send a request by e-mail to the coordinator.

Lone Star Literary Life’s Top Ten Books of 2017

 

It’s that time again — Top Ten time, that is. Time to reflect on the literary year past as we consider which new Texas-related books (or books by Texas authors) to wrap up as gifts for our best bookish friends and family.

 

As always, the team at Lone Star Literary Life hopes you’ll agree with us that Texas is in many ways its own nation of books — great titles in every genre, great authors, great bookstores and publishers worth supporting.

 

And here they are, with links to those we’ve reviewed so far:

 

Fiction

​

  • CALLING MY NAME by Liara Tamani
  • THE SHADOWS WE KNOW BY HEART by Jennifer Park
  • THE GIRLS OF THE GOLDEN WEST: A NOVEL by James Ward Lee
  • ALL THE WIND IN THE WORLD by Samantha Mabry
  • THE NEON PALM OF MADAME MELANÇON by Will Clarke
  • MOXIE: A NOVEL by Jennifer Matheiu
  • IN THE VALLEY OF THE SUN: A NOVEL by Andy Davidson
  • THE BOOK OF POLLY: A NOVEL by Kathy Hepinstall
  • THE GRAVE TENDER by Eliza Maxwell
  • THE INEXPLICABLE LOGIC OF MY LIFE by Benjamin Alire Sáenz

 

Nonfiction

  • TEXAS BLOOD: SEVEN GENERATIONS AMONG THE OUTLAWS, RANCHERS, INDIANS, MISSIONARIES, SOLDIERS, AND SMUGGLERS OF THE BORDERLANDS by Roger D. Hodge
  • THE LAST SHERIFF IN TEXAS: A TRUE TALE OF VIOLENCE AND THE VOTE by James P. McCollom
  • ACCIDENTAL ACTIVISTS: MARK PHARISS, VIC HOLMES, AND THEIR FIGHT FOR MARRIAGE EQUALITY IN TEXAS by David Collins
  • HOUSE BUILT ON ASHES: A MEMOIR by José Antonio Rodríguez
  • SHOOT LIKE A GIRL: ONE WOMAN’S DRAMATIC FIGHT IN AFGHANISTAN AND ON THE HOME FRONT by Mary Jennings Hegar
  • WELCOME HOMELESS: ONE MAN’S JOURNEY OF DISCOVERING THE MEANING OF HOME by Alan Graham
  • DIVIDED WE STAND: THE BATTLE OVER WOMEN’S RIGHTS AND FAMILY VALUES THAT POLARIZED AMERICAN POLITICS by Marjorie J. Spruill
  • SEEING TEXAS HISTORY: THE BOB BULLOCK TEXAS STATE HISTORY MUSEUM, with introduction by Victoria Ramirez and Jan Bullock
  • BLUE TEXAS: THE MAKING OF A MULTIRACIAL DEMOCRATIC COALITION IN THE CIVIL RIGHTS ERA by Max Krochmal
  • LAS NALGAS DE JLO/JLO’S BOOTY: THE BEST AND MOST NOTORIOUS CALUMNAS & OTHER WRITINGS BY THE FIRST CHICANA COLUMNIST IN TEXAS 1995-2005 by Bárbara Renaud Gonzaléz
