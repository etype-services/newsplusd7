Chad Cartwright was born in San Clemente, California on January 29, 1973 to Gary and Zandra Cartwright. He is an author as well as a father, husband, pharmacist, triathlete and entrepreneur.

7.30.17

FICTION

Chad Cartwright

Shock

Paperback, 978-0-9975-4710-8, 2016; ebook 978-0-9975-4712-2, 2017

 

 

Life can knock us down hard and leave us writhing in defeat and self-pity. But, often, a wise relative, friend or mentor may help us stand up again and get back to normal living.

 

But what if we get slammed down painfully a second time, and this time no one is around to help us? Can we find the inner strengths we need to pull ourselves up and move forward?

 

These are important themes in Shock, an intriguing novel by Lubbock author Chad Cartwright. Written mostly in the form of a personal memoir (with echoes of some possibly real-life events), Shock tells the wide-ranging story of Rick Rousser, a young man raised by strict but fair parents in West Texas soon after the Vietnam War.

 

Rick’s “normal” childhood in Lubbock (“the hometown of Buddy Holly, Texas Tech University, and prairie dogs”) follows the storied pattern that points toward success once he graduates from high school. He goes to Tech and the University of Texas at Austin, becomes a pharmacist, and marries his college sweetheart. His dream is to have a family and settle down. But that dream quickly is shattered by shocking circumstances, and Rick lurches into divorce, followed by numbing despair, depression, and drunkenness.

 

Desperate to change his bad luck, he sets out on a mostly aimless journey to Hawaii, Tahiti, and Bali. On that final island and nearly broke, he meets “Coke,” a dive-shop owner who has endured much tougher times in life than Rick. Coke befriends Rick, gives him a job as a diver, encourages him to start training for triathlons, and counsels him: “A failed marriage is not a tragedy. It sucks, but it’s not a tragedy.”

 

Rick later returns to Lubbock, gets a part-time pharmacy job, becomes a history major in graduate school at Tech, and finds new love. This time a tragedy does hit, and it comes from a totally unexpected direction, triggered by his master’s thesis. How Rick handles the deadly crisis is startling, yet believable, and he gets life-boosting support from those who love him, from recalling Coke’s wisdoms, and from pushing himself to compete in triathlons as Coke had urged.

 

Chad Cartwright’s first book could have benefited from tighter editing, closer proofreading, and a more formalized structure. For example, the main character’s name, Rick Rousser, is not clearly stated until page 37. And a temporary and necessary — but unexpected — jump from Rick’s voice to the internal thoughts of his grandfather happens fairly late in the book. The reader’s first impression can be that we suddenly have started reading another book.

 

These pointers aside, Shock is well written, with a bittersweet yet uplifting ending. Cartwright’s storytelling skills bode well for future works.
