Author interviews by
Kay Ellington, LSLL Publisher

 

Each week Lone Star Literary profiles a newsmaker in Texas books and letters, including authors, booksellers, publishers.

 

Kay Ellington has worked in management for a variety of media companies, including Gannett, Cox Communications, Knight-Ridder, and the New York Times Regional Group, from Texas to New York to California to the Southeast and back again to Texas. She is the coauthor, with Barbara Brannon, of the Texas novels The Paragraph Ranch and A Wedding at the Paragraph Ranch.

FRANCES BRANNEN VICK is the retired director and co-founder of the University of North Texas Press and E-Heart Press. She holds the BA in English from the University of Texas at Austin and the and MA in English Stephen F. Austin State University, and an honorary Doctor of Humane Letters from the University of North Texas. After a career teaching English, she published over two hundred books, many of them state and national award winners.

 

In retirement, she has written One Hundred Years of “The Eyes of Texas.” She co-authored Petra’s Legacy: The South Texas Ranching Empire of Petra Vela and Mifflin Kenedy, which won the Coral Horton Tullis Award for the best book on Texas history from Texas State Historical Association, among other awards. She was the Carl Hertzog Award and Lecture Monograph recipient for Confessions of a Texas Publisher/Writer. She produced Literary Dallas and has written introductions and chapters for such books as Texas Women Writers; The Family Saga: A Collection of Texas Family Legends; Texas Women on the Cattle Trails; and Notes from Texas Writers. Her latest books are Letters to Alice: Birth of the Kleberg-King Ranch Dynasty and Tales of Texas Cooking: Stories and Recipes from the Trans-Pecos to the Piney Woods and High Plains to the Gulf Prairies, Texas Folklore Society Publication LXX.

 

 

ELLEN CLARKE TEMPLE is former owner and president of Ellen C. Temple Publishing, Inc. She graduated in 1964 from the University of Texas at Austin, where she received the BA degree. In 1974, she received the MA degree from Stephen F. Austin State University in Nacogdoches. She also completed the Rice Publishing Course in 1979 at Rice University.

 

A longtime advocate for libraries and the humanities in education, Temple has served as a board member for the T.L.L. Temple Memorial Library in Diboll, the Museum of East Texas, the advisory council of the University of Texas at Austin Graduate School of Library and Information Science, and the editorial advisory board of the University of North Texas Press, and has been a member of the Summerlee Commission on Texas history and the Philosophical Society of Texas. Temple was on the Texas Committee for the Humanities from 1983 to 1989 and chaired the committee in 1988. She was appointed to the Board of Regents of the University of Texas System by Governor Ann Richards in 1991.

 

3.5.2017  “There’s just so much a woman can do”—but that's  heck of a lot! Ellen Temple and Fran Vick on writing and publishing Texas women's history

 

Every March for the past quarter of a century, the Texas State Historical Association has recognized the field of Texas women’s history with the Liz Carpenter Award for the best book on the subject. Fran Brannen Vick and Ellen C. Temple are among the groundbreaking historians and publishers who helped plant that field—and it's only fitting that here at the close of another TSHA annual meeting and the outset of another Women’s History Month, we correspond with Fran and Ellen (via email) about the past and future of the field, and their signal contributions to it.

 

LONE STAR LITERARY LIFE: Ellen and Fran, the two of you have been a force—and oftentimes, collaborators—in Texas letters, humanities, and history for more than four decades. How did you meet, and what was the first creative endeavor you worked on together?

 

ELLEN C. TEMPLE: Fran Vick and I met in 1960 in the bleachers at Clark Field while we watched an intramural softball game at the University of Texas at Austin, and found we shared a love for books and writing, and all things UT. We’ve been friends and colleagues ever since.  When we found ourselves together in Lufkin, we worked on our masters’ at SFASU at the same time! Books and writing continued to be our shared passion. Our first collaboration in publishing was the publication of a series of five middle-school readers by Judy Alter that we called the Maggie Books. We worked together to start the Texas Book Publishers Association in the early ’80s, also.

 

 

You discovered a niche in regional publishing in the mid-1980s to mid-’90s. What was it that persuaded you to parlay your experience as researchers and writers into being editors and publishers?

 

ELLEN: Fran was the first to publish a book, a Texas Folklore Society Classic, Built in Texas. At about the same time, in 1980, I attended a two-week Rice Publishing Course, offered by Texas Monthly at Rice University, on book and magazine publishing. I opened up shop in my closet at our home in Diboll and published my first children’s book, Pauline and the Peacock by Doris Daniel. And thinking I was another Alfred Knopf, I named my company Ellen C. Temple Publishing, Inc.! Becoming a publisher just seemed like a natural progression for me, and the Rice Publishing Course offered me a path to do it; it was either that or open a bookstore. I knew I’d have more flexibility with a publishing business, so that’s what I chose to do.

 

FRANCES BRANNEN VICK: I was never a writer until I retired from publishing. Before publishing I was an English teacher. I began publishing by publishing the Texas Folklore Society Publications. The first book I published was Built in Texas in 1979. That was followed by Legendary Ladies of Texas, co-published by the TFS and Texas Foundation for Women's Resources, Women in Texas History Project, with Mary Beth Rogers at the helm.

 

That was my first work with that group, and it led to me distributing their other publications, such as Texas Women: A Celebration of History by Mary Beth Rogers, Ruthe Weingarten, and Sherry Smith. I also distributed We Can Fly: Stories of Kathryn Stinson and other Gutsy Texas Women by Mary Beth Rogers, Sherry Smith, and Janelle Scott—which Ellen brought out under the Ellen C. Temple Publishing imprint. And I also distributed Ellen’s books, which she has mentioned, so I could put together a catalog of enough books to show to the bookstores.

 

To get back to the Texas folklore Society, however. Publishing their books was the hook on which my whole publishing career was hung. It gave me an entree to the writers who were members of the Society. Jim Lee brought me his Classics of Texas Fiction; F. E. Abernethy brought Singing' Texas; Archie McDonald brought Helpful Household Hints for HouseHusbands of Uppity Women, for three examples. There were many others. All of the Texas Folklore Society books are now under the UNT Press umbrella after I collected them there when I went to UNT to help start the university press there. The breakout book was Legendary Ladies of Texas. We also went into second printings with The Family Saga: A Collection of Texas Family Legends and a book I edited Tales of Texas Cooking: Stories and Recipes from the Trans-Pecos to the Piney Woods and High Plains to the Gulf Prairies in 2016. That book went into second printing almost immediately after release! That was a first for a TFS book, I think.

 

 

Was there a particular breakout book for either of you?

 

ELLEN: Two of my most important books were Texas women’s history: Citizens at Last: The Woman Suffrage Movement in Texas, edited by Ruthe Winegarten and Judith MacArthur (1987) and Jane Y. McCallum: Diaries and Writings of a Texas Suffragist by Janet Humphrey (1988).

 

My breakout book was The Train to Estelline, an epistolary novel by Jane Roberts Wood (1987). All three books are still in print. I went on to sell the paperback rights for Train to Bantam Doubleday Dell for national distribution. Interesting to note that the Dallas Morning News had an excellent review column and even a bestsellers list each Sunday, so I targeted Jane’s hometown, Dallas, for marketing and sales. Fran distributed for me through her E-Heart Press and made sure that the books stayed in stores. Train stayed on the bestseller list, and Bantam noticed it and got in touch with me to ask to buy paper rights. So I always thank Fran for keeping Train in the stores in Dallas!

 

 

How has publishing changed over the years you’ve been involved—particularly in regard to regional presses and university presses?

 

ELLEN: When we started the Book Publishers Association in 1980s, there were several independent presses but most are no longer in business. Distribution challenges, bookstore policies on returns, property taxes on inventory—all played a role. University Presses are now the main publishers in Texas, and they do a great job for Texas writers.

 

FRAN: Publishing has changed dramatically in that the number of presses that were operating in Texas when Ellen and I first started publishing are no longer in existence. Ellen spoke of the Book Publishers Association that we belonged to. It is no longer in existence because the many presses that started the association are no longer in business. Cinco Puntos Press in El Paso is one of the few of that era still going very strong. However, the university presses in Texas have taken up the slack of publishing Texas writers and Texas topics left by the demise of the small presses of yore. Texas A&M University Press has put together a consortium of university presses that market all of the books together in the consortium. I would not have been able to start UNT Press without the distribution that the Texas A&M Press Consortium offered. I think distribution may have been one of the major problems with the small presses when I first started publishing. It is almost impossible to get around to all the bookstores in the state at the time, then and now, by one small press. The Texas A&M Press Consortium offers that and they also present all the books in the catalog to the corporate offices of Barnes & Noble, Borders Buc-cee’s, for example. They have national and international book reps covering North America, including entrees to Costco, Sams, and shops/stores covered by umbrella companies—gift stores, tourist attractions, etc. They cover the wholesale distributors such as Ingram and Baker & Taylor.

 

Also, one of the big changes would have to be e-books. That has opened up a whole new area of publishing and placement for books.

 

 

What books did you each read growing up? Did you come from a family of storytellers? What inspired you to pursue literature and history as a vocation and avocation?

 

FRAN: I read any book I could get my hands on growing up. I started very young with a group of books I think called Land of Enchantment, that perhaps came with the Books of Knowledge. At least they were all kept together in one place. I can’t be sure of the names of those sets, however, and I have no one left to ask. Anyway, I loved them.

 

I read early because I would not stay home with the babysitter while my mother went to teach, and ran away enough times that my mother decided I had better go to school her. I was three—not old enough to be wandering around Groveton. Mother was teaching an elementary class at Pennington. I don’t know the exact grade. I could stay if I sat in the back of the room and kept my mouth shut! So I began learning by osmosis as she taught, I suppose. I do not remember not being able to read. Plus everyone in the family was reading all the time, so I would be left out if I didn’t read!

 

One of the “fairy tales” I read and loved because of the wonderful pictures accompanying it, did not have a name, or not one that I knew at the time or could understand. When I was a senior in high school, however, I discovered while reading one of our assignments that the story I had read when I was very, very young was a child’s version of Beowulf. I told my teacher, “I know this story. I know how it ends.” He asked me to tell him about it and I did and finally remembered when I had first read the story. You would think that would warp my young mind, right? Maybe it did, but I loved those scary drawings and the story.

 

Yes, I came from a family of marvelous storytellers—both of my parents were incredible teachers and parents. My father filled me full of family stories. I spent a lot of time in his lap when I was very young, begging him to tell me another story. My mother was the most colorful and imaginative person in my world, and in a lot of other people’s worlds, too. She made up words for things, for example. And would do such things as put out a saucer of milk on the back step in the evening and say, “If you want your flowers to be gay and bright, feed the fairies by the bright moonlight.” I know now she was probably feeding stray cats, but the fairies were very real to me. Need I say she was Irish?

 

ELLEN: My mother was a librarian, a pioneer in the field, and she directed my reading.  I read everything! All the time! When she urged me to apply for a summer job with the local library in Lufkin, and I worked there for seven summers, my fate was sealed. I went on to major in English and history at the University of Texas at Austin. My favorite possession in those years was a stack permit for the main library in the Tower! I love to write, to read, to publish, and I love the people who do it, and also the people who sell books and who serve as librarians.

 

 

During Women’s History Month, it’s only fitting that we talk about Houston in 1977 and the women’s marches earlier this year. Many books and talks will commemorate the National Women’s Conference in Houston in 1977. But maybe you could tell our readers—many of a generation too young to remember this event—what happened in 1977, and why it is so important to uncover and tell the stories of women’s history.

 

ELLEN:  Here’s my story of the National Women’s History Conference, 1977. I started writing a history column for my small-town Diboll Free Press in 1974. For eight years, our family traveled back and forth between Diboll and Austin, so I had the opportunity to do research in both places. I was lucky enough to discover an essay by A. Elizabeth Taylor entitled “The Woman Suffrage Movement in Texas” (Journal of Southern History 27:2, May 1951, 194–215). I was a history major without any knowledge of the one-hundred-year struggle for the vote other than Susan B. Anthony’s lead role.

 

So for my first history column for the Free Press, armed with Dr. Taylor’s essay, I wrote the “History of the Woman Suffrage Movement in Angelina County.” I wanted to know the local story—especially how our local representatives had voted (no). The editor gave me a full page! And I went on to write about Fannie Farrington, who brought “civilization” to the rough-and-tumble sawmill town of Diboll; about Cora Nash, who cooked for the workers at the boardinghouses; about the Rev. Emma Irick, who was the minister for the Church of the Nazarene in Lufkin, and many others.

 

Two events gave my collection of history articles a name. I attended the state run-up conference in Austin in preparation for the National Women’s Conference to convene in Houston in 1977. In Austin, I attended the women’s history session (the first time I had heard the term, “women’s history”). There were about ten of us—and since no leader showed up, we talked. And I realized that I was writing women’s history for the Free Press. When I attended the national conference in Houston, I went as a representative of the Free Press, with press badge and all.

 

Knowing our history opens up a whole new world for women, confirming that we had just as much  a role in shaping the history of our state and nation as our husbands, brothers, and fathers have had.  That knowledge empowers us.

 

FRAN: I was not there [in Houston, 1977] . . . however, everyone was influenced by it. The Foundation for Women’s Resources started the Leadership Texas program for women in 1983. I imagine that came out of the Conference or was influenced by it. I was in the Leadership Texas class of 1990, the year Ann Richards was running for governor. Between the educational presentations given to us and the election, it was a heady year. Our class bonded so strongly that we still meet for reunions every year. We have not missed a year. It is a great program.

 

 

What stories of Texas women are still waiting to be researched and told?

 

FRAN: There are so many stories to be told. I think there is an enormous amount to be done on the Tejanas. I fell in love with South Texas working on Petra’s Legacy with Jane Monday, who had spent years in the area researching the vaqueros, Petra, the ranches—the whole area and has written many books about them. She opened up a whole new world to me. I am working with Jane and her husband, Charles, on a book about Dr. A. E. Spohn, who has an incredible story as a country doctor in Corpus Christi, and was married to Petra’s daughter, Sarah.

 

ELLEN: We have only scratched the surface of the stories that need to be told about Texas women.  I’ll refer your readers to three groundbreaking titles that suggest topics: Women and Texas History: Selected Essays, edited by Fane Downs and Nancy Baker Jones (TSHA, 1993); Texas Through Women’s Eyes: The Twentieth-Century Experience by Judith McArthur and Harold Smith (UT Press, 2010); and Texas Women: Their Histories, Their Lives, edited by Elizabeth Hayes Turner, Stephanie Cole, and Rebecca Sharpless (University of Georgia Press, 2015). I’d also urge your readers to read about the discovery of Texas women’s history in “Making Texas Our Texas: The Emergence of Texas Women’s History, 1976-1990,” by Nancy Baker Jones (Southwestern Historical Quarterly, 120:3, Jan. 2017).

 

 

Which Texas women do you most admire?

 

ELLEN: The Texas women I most admire are Lady Bird Johnson, Ann Richards, Liz Carpenter, and Fran Vick.

 

FRAN [who doesn’t know Ellen has just answered this!]: Historically, the first woman I most admire has to be my mother, an incredible woman. There is a school named for her in Lake Jackson, Texas, Bess Brannen Elementary School—that’s how good a teacher she was and how much she affected the students under her. It was such a privilege to have been raised by her. She was an independent woman, who rarely went by Mrs. C. A. Brannen. Her name was Bess C. Brannen, and she had her own checking account at a time when I don't think many married women did. None of that bothered my father, either. He expected me to be independent, too. So I had good influences from both parents.

 

 

What advice do you have for apprentice writers?

 

FRAN: Keep writing. Don’t stop. Find something that intrigues you and go after it.

 

ELLEN: My advice for writers today: Write Texas Women’s History!

 

 

What’s next for both of you?

 

ELLEN:  The good lord willing, I’ll keep forging ahead.

 

FRAN: When the A. E. Spohn book gets put to bed, Jane has another project in mind. She told me yesterday that she has two projects in mind! I hope I live long enough to get all of it done. I had thought I would try to get some of my family history down, but I may just have to collect notes on that and let one of my children take care of writing it. There’s just so much a woman can do!
